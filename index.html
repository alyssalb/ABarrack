About

Who I Am

A dramaturg's job is to ask questions, identify and encourage friction, and reveal underlying structures and patterns. Dramaturgy guides my practice across the board, and forms my fundamental ethos through my work including digitally and pedagogically.

I'm a PhD candidate in the Texts and Technology program at UCF, where I work at the intersection of dramaturgy, digital humanities, critical and political analysis, and accessible technology pedagogy. I also hold an MA in Interdisciplinary Studies (audience studies in film, theatre, and video games) and dual BAs in Theatre Arts and Professional Writing, Rhetoric, and Technology from USF.

In my youth I was extremely plugged into digital culture — making things, writing, participating in fan spaces, and connecting with digital community. As a scholar and professional now, I've gained some useful critical distance. I observe some big downsides to corporate digital control more clearly now. However, I can also see the beauty and power of all the things an untrained person can make and do online — through DIY methods, community awareness and connection, open-source tools, generative AI and LLMs, and a basic willingness to have ideas and learn how to make them happen.

That tension — between digital forms as equalizers and argumentative tools in scholarship and citizenship, and their tendency to not act as such — is where my work currently predominantly lives. I'm driven by the distinctly political historical and current practice of dramaturgy, and by a belief that the tools of digital production are methods as much as they are tools for rhetorical argumentation and expression.

I bring these ideas into both my scholarship and my teaching. In my research, I use dramaturgy to reveal and consider pieces of digital culture and relate them contextually to the things we experience artistically and theatrically. In my teaching, I aim to empower others to learn and use the digital tools available to them for good. I'm an artist, a scholar, and a teacher, and I understand those roles as versions of the same work.
