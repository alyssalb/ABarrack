Overview

The Course

This course explores dramaturgy as a profession, an act, and a mode of inquiry, looking at how it can facilitate meaning-making, ask questions, and shape artistic processes.

This upper-division course introduces students to dramaturgy through both its traditional tools (script analysis, research, contextual framing) and its life as an experimental, critical, and creative practice. We work across text, performance, media, and design, treating dramaturgy less as a fixed job description and more as a habit of inquiry that shapes how a production comes to mean something.

I built the course around Michael Mark Chemers' Ghost Light as a conceptual spine (roughly one chapter per week) and paired each week of theory with a day of hands-on application. Students read about dramaturgy and then apply what they've learned, practicing quick research skills, writing and preparing other communicative forms for real potential collaborators and audiences, and accumulating a portfolio of usable materials to showcase their dramaturgical work and ethos (through a personal dramaturgy manifesto assignment).

Course Design

How It Works

Theory Monday, Practice Friday

Mondays build foundations through pre-class reading and discussion; Fridays transform Monday discussions into a concrete deliverable through in-class exercises, including simulated director meetings, in-class research and synthesis, and a collective actor packet.

Deliverables Become a Portfolio

In-class exercises are designed to accumulate. By the end of the term, students hold a body of collaborator- and audience-facing materials and a personal dramaturgy manifesto.

Tied to a Live Production

Early weeks connected to a real experimental production in the department, staged at UCF Celebrates the Arts 2026. Students' research fed directly into the show's devising process, and their work reached real audiences.

Experimental & Digital Dramaturgy

A dedicated experimental project invites digital or analogue audience materials, and a digital-dramaturgy unit (Thomas; Zuckerberg) asks what technology does (and doesn't) do for the field.

Production Spotlight · UCF Celebrates the Arts 2026

Early weeks of this section connected to a real experimental production within the department, performed at UCF Celebrates the Arts 2026 at the Dr. Phillips Center. Students, in collaboration with me and each other, built annotated bibliographies that led to our collective dramaturgy packet materials, which contributed heavily to the devising process for that production (which I was additionally involved in). One dramaturgy student in this course presented some of their course work as a pre-show engagement activity for audiences during the real production.

Learning Outcomes

What Students Learn

  • Analyze the historical and professional foundations of dramaturgy through weekly readings and discussion.
  • Apply core dramaturgical skills through script analysis, research tasks, collaborative exercises, and audience engagement.
  • Design experimental dramaturgy materials (digital or analogue) that communicate ideas clearly to audiences.
  • Create a final dramaturgy project synthesizing research, artistic choices, and social/community context.

The Semester

Course Arc

Wks 1–3 Foundations

What is dramaturgy, and how do we research fast and share cleanly? A "crash course" through the director meeting, source research, and the actor packet, building an annotated bibliography, revising it, and reading Chemers and Elinor Fuchs' "Visit to a Small Planet."

Wks 4–8 History, Theory & Critical Lens

History and theories of dramaturgy, power and the critical lens, technical analysis, and "form follows function," working through Ghost Light chapters alongside the experimental dramaturgy proposal and the "why this play now?" question.

Wks 9–12 New Work, Collaboration & the Digital

New plays and adaptation, collaboration, and digital dramaturgy (Thomas; Zuckerberg's "Can Technology Save Theatre?"). Experimental dramaturgy materials come together in an informal show-and-tell.

Wks 13–16 Synthesis & Showcase

Finishing Ghost Light, drafting a personal dramaturgy manifesto (via Loveless' "How to Make Art at the End of the World"), and developing the final project through iterative feedback toward a two-day final showcase of presentations.

AI Literacy

AI in This Course

Dramaturgy is a natural home for experimenting with AI as a collaborator. Here, AI literacy is treated as a fundamental skill, with disclosure and human authorship as non-negotiables.

AI use is required for this course. We'll experiment with and discuss AI tools to build your AI literacy and comfort, and help you feel empowered as a scholar/artist using AI tools in your work. AI can be super helpful for a wide variety of tasks, and unhelpful for others — we'll learn some helpful uses and how to verify and credit appropriately.

Since this class is built for AI tool experimentation, make sure to tell me what tool you're using and how you used it! I value your curiosity. Show up with questions, ideas, and concerns, and feel free to set up time to discuss things in more detail with me.
