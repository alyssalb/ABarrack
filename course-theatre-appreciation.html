Overview

The Course

An introduction to theatre as an art form and a living practice.

Theatre Appreciation surveys theatre history as a set of forms, a form of storytelling and expression, and a function in the living world. The course covers the people, disciplines, and capacity for social change involved in the art form of theatre. The course is fully online and asynchronous and ran on an eight-week condensed term calendar, though I have taught in-person both as a hybrid condensed term course and as a fully in-person full term course as well, with slight variations on the syllabus and more in-class interactive activities. Students engage with readings, plays, recorded content, and a range of creative and reflective assignments. No prior experience with theatre is required or assumed.

Designing for an online, intro-level, mixed-experience audience shaped every choice here. I wanted the course to feel like a real classroom even without a shared room, so it's built on low-stakes reflection, creative weekly activities, genuine community guidelines, and a final project students shape to their own interests and strengths.

Course Design

How It Works

Weekly Check-Ins

Each week requires a short, unscripted video or audio reflection on the week's activities and content. This acts as a running reflective journal, keeping personal ideas and embodiment present in the async mode.

Creative, Flexible Activities

Most weeks include a making task, such as a spectatorship map, a genre/stylistic mood board, an annotated scene, and more. Format is open to student strengths, and options/ideas are listed for each activity to allow students to work in a medium that fits them.

Final Project

Students pick a format such as a digital presentation, video essay, annotated digital collection, podcast, or a proposed alternative, and work with a play from the semester to demonstrate understanding of how a play gets from text/script to mounted production in their chosen style.

Community-Centric

Explicit community guidelines are provided to guide engagement with peers and instructor. Discussion boards are built around seeing and responding to one another's creative work.

Learning Outcomes

What Students Learn

The course is built around one central question: what does it take to make a performance happen? We return to it all semester through the plays we read, the history we cover, and the projects students make. The undercurrent is that theatre is an ancient, collective act: every performance, from a Greek chorus to a WWE match to a school play, depends on a network of visible and invisible labor, and expands or contracts what counts as "theatre" depending on who's watching and who's making.

  • Identify multiple forms of theatre and performance across historical periods and cultural contexts.
  • Explain the function of key roles involved in producing a live performance.
  • Trace what it would take to move a play or performance idea from page/concept to live performance.
  • Articulate your own relationship to theatre and performance, including where you've encountered it in your life.

The Reading List

Plays We Read

  • Antigone, Sophocles
  • A Midsummer Night's Dream, Shakespeare
  • She Kills Monsters, Qui Nguyen

All three plays and the open-access course textbook (Theatre Appreciation by Kiara Pipino) are provided free in Falcon Online, with no textbook cost to students.

The Eight Weeks

Course Arc

Wks 1–2 What Is Theatre?

What theatre is, spaces and spectatorship, how to read a play, and genres and styles. Students map a performance space they've experienced and practice reading a script toward staging. Antigone is assigned.

Wks 3–4 The Makers & Design

The people who make theatre (acting, directing, producing, playwriting) and the design disciplines of set, costume, lighting, and sound. Students respond to Antigone, the final-project proposal takes shape, and A Midsummer Night's Dream is assigned.

Wks 5–6 History & Applied Theatre

A brief history of theatre, then applied theatre, theatre for social change, and musicals. Students compare historical eras and profile a real applied-theatre example; She Kills Monsters is assigned.

Wks 7–8 Contemporary Forms & Capstone

Contemporary and global theatre, then a wrap-up week focused on completing the self-directed final project. Students respond to She Kills Monsters and reflect on how their definition of theatre has changed.

AI Literacy

AI in This Course

I believe AI literacy is a valuable skill, and I'm open to exploring how these tools intersect with theatre and creative work. If you're curious about using AI in this course, I encourage that curiosity as long as you're being thoughtful and critical about your use.

A few ground rules: you must be the primary author of your own thinking, analysis, and creative work. AI can support your process (research starter, brainstorming, designing ideas), but it can't replace your voice or judgment. If you use AI for any part of an assignment, disclose it briefly (tool used, what it generated, how you revised or vetted it). Not sure if a use case fits? Ask me before you use it; I'd rather talk it through with you than guess after the fact.
