Philosophy

Teaching Philosophy

Knowing how something works is more useful than having it done for you.

My courses work toward an active and flipped classroom. Students have theory/ideas in hand when they get to class and arrive ready to use class time for practice, peer work, discussion, and guided discovery. I love including ample hands-on activities with instructor and peer guidance and support. I also encourage critique and critical, respectful disagreement in interpreting ideas. To this end, I also encourage resource sharing, a communal agreement on behaviors and decorum in the room, and lively discussion in class.

A significant thread in my teaching (as well as my scholarship) is digital and media literacy, in particular right now AI literacy. I'm not interested in telling students whether or not they should regularly use AI tools, but I am invested in teaching them how to evaluate new tools, how to understand how they work and what they're doing, and how to make informed, ethics-driven, practical decisions about whether these tools are useful for their individual scholarship and practice. Generative AI and LLMs are here, and it is crucial in my opinion to equip students to encounter these in the modern workplace, whatever that workplace may be.

Finally, I'm an advocate for practice-based learning, especially in theatre and digital media. The best way to understand these fields is to make things, both individually and collaboratively.

Sample AI Policies

My AI policy adapts for each course depending on the constraints and needs of the material, context, and student population I'm addressing. Across the board, I aim to encourage literacy and responsibility and avoid negative and prohibitive statements. AI can stay in a supporting role or in more digital-forward courses can become a more significant part of our work, up to being a requirement. Below is some sample language that I use when a course is designed at least in part to teach AI fluency more directly:

AI use is required for this course. We'll experiment with and discuss AI tools to build your AI literacy and comfort, and help you feel empowered as a scholar/artist using AI tools in your work. AI can be super helpful for a wide variety of tasks, and unhelpful for others — we'll learn some helpful uses and how to verify and credit appropriately.

Since this class is built for AI tool experimentation, make sure to tell me what tool you're using and how you used it! I value your curiosity. Show up with questions, ideas, and concerns, and feel free to set up time to discuss things in more detail with me.

And a lighter-touch example, from a course where AI stays firmly in a supporting role:

I believe AI literacy is a valuable skill, and I'm open to exploring how these tools intersect with theatre and creative work. If you're curious about using AI in this course, I encourage that curiosity as long as you're being thoughtful and critical about your use.

A few ground rules: you must be the primary author of your own thinking, analysis, and creative work. AI can support your process (research starter, brainstorming, designing ideas), but it can't replace your voice or judgment. If you use AI for any part of an assignment, disclose it briefly (tool used, what it generated, how you revised or vetted it). Not sure if a use case fits? Ask me before you use it; I'd rather talk it through with you than guess after the fact.
