Overview

The Course

A broad overview of the history, present, and future of technical theatre, learned through readings, discussion, and hands-on activities.

Stagecraft explores the technical elements and professions involved in live theatrical events: scenic construction and painting, lighting, sound, props, and stage management, alongside the history that shaped them. Students gain knowledge of each discipline's unique skill set, safety procedures, and creative process, plus a working technical vocabulary for talking about how theatre gets made.

I've designed and taught this course across three terms, refining it each time. Each session moves from lecture and history into a discussion segment before moving on to a hands-on activity or demo, allowing abstract concepts to sink in through making practices. Safety is emphasized at all points and leads throughout the course, from PPE and shop attire to developing and encouraging a culture of care that I hope for students to carry into all production environments.

Course Design

How It Works

Session Rhythm

Each class moves repeatably from lecture/history → short break → deeper discussion or collaborative brainstorm/exploration → hands-on activity of varying levels or a demo.

Emphasis on Safety

PPE, shop attire, and respect for tools and equipment are built into the course literally from day one, including a collaborative safety brainstorm, consistent repetition and recall activities for safety concerns, and repeat demonstrations of safety best practices. The goal is to foster a habit of care, attentiveness, and awareness in students that they can carry to any shop or stage they may work in moving forward.

Learn by Making

Students build model periaktos, sketch and construct a mini flat, paint a wood-grain and stone texture sample, experiment with color gels and lighting systems on an EOS console, draft a cue sheet, and classify props on a scavenger hunt. More hands-on shop activities will be added progressively in future iterations of the course.

In-Built Reflection

Low-stakes quizzes emphasize review and offer a place for discussion starters, and the course closes with a final reflective paper connecting course concepts and activities to real productions students have seen or worked on recently.

Learning Outcomes

What Students Learn

  • Describe the technical disciplines and professions in theatrical production.
  • Use technical terminology fluently in a theatre environment.
  • Explain and demonstrate proper safety precautions and procedures in technical theatre spaces.
  • Apply creative and collaborative skills to the design and build process on a small scale.
  • Understand major historical developments in stagecraft and their impact on modern theatre.
  • Discuss theatre through a technical lens, as developed in a final reflective paper.

The Semester

Course Arc

Unit 1 Orientation & the Stage as Workplace

Safety, shop culture, and a working vocabulary (stage geography, theatre types, soft goods, and structures), grounded in a collaborative hazard-and-mitigation brainstorm.

Unit 2 A History of Technical Theatre

A survey of important technical theatre historical progression from Greek and Roman innovation (theatron, skene, machinery, periaktoi) through medieval and Renaissance staging (pageant wagons, perspective scenery, Serlio) to the Baroque chariot-and-pole system, the proscenium arch, and 19th-century realism.

Unit 3 Tools, Scenery & Paint

Shop tools, hardware, and materials; scenic design fundamentals (scale, proportion, ground plans and elevations, common constructed pieces); and scenic painting technique, from dilution and brushwork to color theory.

Unit 4 Lighting, Sound & Props

Lighting fundamentals (the controllable qualities of light, color mixing, fixtures, and angles), sound systems and cueing, and props (categories, sourcing ethics, storage, and preset practice).

Unit 5 Management, Careers & Reflection

Stage management and paperwork, tech careers and industry trends (unions, sustainability, digital tools), budget-conscious and reuse-driven design, and a final-paper workshop that closes the loop between concept and lived production.
